2.3 Evolution of Tourism

2.3.1 International vs. Domestic Tourism

  • international tourism: tourism crossing national boundaries

  • domestic tourism: tourism within a country

    2.3.2 Mass vs. Niche Tourism

  • mass tourism: large numbers of people visiting a particular destination

  • niche tourism: tourism surrounding a specific interest, area or activity

    2.3.3 Short-haul vs. Long-haul Tourism

2.4 Growth of Global Tourism

2.4.1 Trends

  • overall growth in tourism number as a result of globalisation
    • 25 million international arrivals in 1950, 903 million in 2008

2.4.2 Reasons

  • worldwide economic prosperity, greater affluence
  • decreasing costs of tourism
  • widening range of destinations
  • expansion of budget airlines
  • retail reward schemes (air miles) aimed at travel and tourism
  • improvements in transportation infrastructure and technology
    • wide-body aircrafts able to carry more passengers, reducing flight costs
  • improved accessibility and transport facilities
    • better airports, reduced air fares, package holidays, computerised reservation systems
  • more leisure time
    • increased number of days of paid leave
  • larger number of retirees, more leisure time
  • prominence of mass media
    • holiday programmes, magazines and brochures promote new and different places and activities
    • raised expectations of international travel with media coverage of holidays, travel and nature
    • online booking increases convenience of air travel and hotel reservation
  • rise of middle class in newly industrialised economies (BRICS)

2.5 Impact of Tourism

2.5.1 Sociocultural Impact

  • rejuvenate and strengthen cultural events and festivals which may have gradually disappeared due to a lack of local interest
    • staged cultural performance in Balinese hotels ensured survival of traditional dance troupes
    • tourist demand revived local interest in artwork and dances

  • ensure preservation and survival of local cultures and traditions

  • facilities developed for tourists can also benefit local residents

  • commercialisation of culture and art
    • attach monetary value to culture
    • conform to tourists' expectations
    • adapting to tourist demands

  • loss of authenticity, staged authenticity

  • can lead to an increase in crime, child labour and flourishing of prostitution for sex tourism

  • intrusion on daily life, loss of privacy, sense of crowding

  • decline of local cultural practices and institutions

  • introduction of new diseases

2.5.2 Economic Impact

  • multiplier effect: money generated by tourism recirculated in economy
    • visitors pay for products, services and experiences (food, accommodation, travel and entertainment)
    • increase in tax revenue from new tourist and local spending can be used to improve infrastructure (education, health), and the creation of jobs
  • source of export earnings
    • tourism is largest export earner
    • help wean economies off over-dependence on limited traditional exports
    • Maldives now relies heavily on tourism sector, previously relied on fisheries sector

  • act as economic "shock absorber" helping to support communities through economic drought

  • decentralised, little dependence on urban centres and imports to sustain tourism activity

  • opportunities for regional development, as a labour intensive industry, can provide employment and training opportunities for young people

  • over-reliance on tourism instead of diversification can draw labour away from staple industries such as agriculture and manufacturing
    • agricultural decline and decline in number of monastery lamas in Nepal
    • 83% of Maldivians dependent on tourism
  • may have high economic leakages: money sent out of country instead of being invested locally, may lead to economic stagnation
    • in Thailand, 70% of all money spent by tourists leave Thailand
  • enclave tourism does not help local businesses
    • Club Med: little benefit to local businesses, no boost to economy
    • cruises: smaller trickle-down effect on local economies
  • inflation: increase in local prices
    • increased demand for goods and services, leads to increased cost of living
    • crowding out effect
    • prices in Belize rose 8%
  • part time and seasonal jobs lead to job insecurity, no guarantee of employment
    • unsatisfactory housing and working conditions

2.5.3 Environmental Impact

  • taxation and tourist resources can be used to fund conservation efforts and scientific research
    • trekkers visiting the Annapurna Circuit trial pay $25.00 fee, money is put towards forest management activities, community development, tourism development and conservation education
      • conservation education in schools, awareness camps, educational material development, clean-up campaigns, natural history museum
  • overcrowding of national parks and reserves has disturbed wildlife, tourist activity detrimental to animals' survival
    • in Kenya, overcrowding of parks such as Tsavo, Masai Mara and Mounr Meru damaged savannah landscape
    • safari jeeps cause soil erosion and scares off wildlife
    • lion population dwindles
  • environmental bubble, unable to cope with fast-rising tourist demands
    • strain on various resources
    • much higher demand for water from tourists
      • golf course in Thailand equivalent to 60 000 rural villagers in water consumption
    • high amount of heated water, electricity to deliver during high season
    • air pollution
  • unsustainable development of environmental tourism has negatively impacted environment
    • improperly developed waste disposal: elephants poisoned by batteries, smaller animals consume rubbish and become ill
    • animals scavenge through garbage rather than hunting naturally
    • bottles thrown haphazardly in Himalayas, stuck there forever
    • cruise ships in Caribbean produce > 70 000 tons of waste
    • sewage run off can lead to coral damage, eutrophication
    • loss of biodiversity
  • construction of tourist facilities have led to destruction of landscape/habitat
    • construction of tourist accommodation and facilities along Himalayas in Nepal caused soil instability and slope failure
    • deforestation in Himalayas for energy needs, resulted in devastating landslides from soil erosion
